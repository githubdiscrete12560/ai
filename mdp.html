Understanding Subjects, Verbs, and Pronouns

1. Subject-Verb Agreement

This is the most important rule of grammar! It simply means the subject (who or what is doing the action) must match the verb (the action).

The Main Rule: Singular subjects get singular verbs. Plural subjects get plural verbs.

In the present tense, singular verbs often end in "s" (for he/she/it/a single person). Plural verbs do not.

  • The student studies. (Singular)
  • She runs. (Singular)
  • The dog barks. (Singular)
  • The students study. (Plural)
  • They run. (Plural)
  • The dogs bark. (Plural)

2. What is a Pronoun?

A pronoun is a "shortcut" word. It replaces a noun (a person, place, or thing) to avoid repeating the same noun over and over.

  • Instead of "The student... The student... The student..."
  • You say: "The student studies. He is tired." (or She, or They)

Common pronouns include: I, you, he, she, it, we, they, me, him, her, us, them.

3. The Singular Gender-Neutral "They"

This is a very common and useful part of modern English! The word "they" can be used as a singular pronoun.

We use it when:

  • The gender of the person is unknown.
  • The gender of the person is not relevant.
  • You want to avoid the clunky "he or she".

Look at this sentence from our last exercise:

"When the student browses, they become tired."

Here, "student" is singular, but we use "they" because we don't know (or care) if the student is male or female. "They" is being used as a singular, gender-neutral pronoun.

Wait, but what about verbs?

This is the tricky part! Even when "they" is used for one person, it still takes the plural verb form (the one without the "s").

  • "The student... they become tired." (Correct)
  • "The student... they becomes tired." (Incorrect)
